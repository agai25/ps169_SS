THE DRAFTING OF THE CONSTITUTION

THE CONSTITUTIONAL CONVENTION

In the summer of 1787, a special meeting called the Constitutional Convention took place in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. Fifty-five men from twelve states (Rhode Island didn't send anyone) came together to create a new plan for America's government. They met in the Pennsylvania State House, which is now called Independence Hall - the same building where the Declaration of Independence was signed eleven years earlier.

THE PEOPLE WHO WROTE THE CONSTITUTION

The men who wrote the Constitution were called the Framers. Some important Framers were:

  • George Washington from Virginia, who led the meeting
  • James Madison from Virginia, who took detailed notes and suggested many ideas
  • Benjamin Franklin from Pennsylvania, who was the oldest person there at 81 years old
  • Alexander Hamilton from New York, who wanted a strong national government

These men were lawyers, farmers, merchants, and leaders in their communities. They were well-educated and wanted to create a better government for the new nation.

WHY THEY NEEDED A NEW PLAN

Before the Constitution, America had a different set of rules called the Articles of Confederation. This plan wasn't working well because:

  • The national government was too weak
  • It couldn't collect taxes to pay its bills
  • It couldn't solve problems between states
  • It couldn't protect American trade with other countries

DISAGREEMENTS ABOUT THE GOVERNMENT

The Framers didn't always agree on how the new government should work. They had big disagreements about:

Size of Government

  • Some people, like Alexander Hamilton, wanted a strong federal (national) government
  • Others, like George Mason, worried that a strong federal government might take away states' rights and individual freedoms

Representation in Congress

  • Large states wanted representation based on population (more people = more votes)
  • Small states wanted equal representation (each state gets the same number of votes)
  • They solved this with the Great Compromise: two parts of Congress - the House of Representatives (based on population) and the Senate (two senators per state)

Slavery

  • Northern states wanted to count only free people for representation
  • Southern states wanted to count enslaved people too
  • They made the Three-Fifths Compromise, which counted each enslaved person as three-fifths of a person for representation (this unfair rule was later changed after the Civil War)

FINISHING THE CONSTITUTION

After four months of debates and compromises, the Framers finished writing the Constitution on September 17, 1787. Thirty-nine of the Framers signed it. Then it had to be approved by at least nine states before it could become the law of the land.

The Constitution created our system of government with three branches - legislative, executive, and judicial - and a system of checks and balances to make sure no part of the government became too powerful.

VOCABULARY

Framers: The people who wrote the Constitution.

Constitutional Convention: The meeting in Philadelphia where the Constitution was written.

Articles of Confederation: The first plan of government for the United States that was replaced by the Constitution.

Compromise: When people give up some of what they want to reach an agreement.

Ratify: To approve or accept officially.
